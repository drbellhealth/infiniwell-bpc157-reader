Infiniwell BPC-157 FAQ

Quick answers to the questions visitors most often ask about Infiniwell BPC-157 Rapid Pro.

What is Infiniwell BPC-157 supposed to do?

It is a one-ingredient capsule — 500 mcg of lab-made BPC-157 — sold for repair and recovery. The two situations it fits best are a stubborn tendon, ligament, or muscle that will not heal, and upper-gut issues like gastritis or NSAID-related irritation. The gut case fits the oral route best, since the peptide originally came from a stomach protein.

What is in the capsule?

Just one active: 500 mcg of BPC-157 in a vegetarian capsule, with no filler and no blend. Infiniwell sells the peptide straight rather than padding the label with extra ingredients. Confirm the exact amount on your bottle.

Does the oral form actually work?

This is the big question. Almost all the strong research used injections, and the capsule is a different route. The fair read is that the pill most likely works in your gut — where the peptide started — plus maybe a bit beyond, and the 'heals a far-away joint' claim is the weakest part. So it is a better fit for a gut goal than for a distant tendon.

How do you take it?

Labels usually suggest one to two capsules a day. For the gut, take one first thing in the morning, about 20 to 30 minutes before food, with a little water. For a tendon or ligament, two a day (morning and then between lunch and dinner), on a fairly empty stomach. Pair it with the actual rehab — it is meant to help you break a plateau, not skip the work.

How long do you stay on it?

Treat it as a short experiment: about four to six weeks on, then two weeks off, aimed at one measurable goal. It is not meant to be a daily forever thing — there is no long-term human data, the rules keep changing, and a compound that grows blood vessels is not one to take endlessly without monitoring.

Who should avoid it?

Clear nos: anyone with cancer now or recently (the blood-vessel mechanism), and anyone pregnant or breastfeeding (no data). Check with your prescriber first if you are on immune-suppressing medication. And do not combine it with high-dose NSAIDs or oral steroids, which work against it.

Is it FDA-approved or even legal?

It is not FDA-approved and it is not a normal dietary supplement. The status is shifting in 2026: BPC-157 came off the FDA's Category 2 list in April 2026 and is up for review for the compounding list in July 2026. Practically, the clean path is the practitioner channel for the capsule and physicians plus compounding pharmacies for the injectable, and the rules will likely keep moving.

Does it replace physical therapy?

No — and that is the biggest thing that decides whether it helps. The people who got something from it were specific about the target and kept up the rehab. The ones who took the capsule and rested usually noticed nothing. Treat it as a six-week test attached to a real recovery plan.
